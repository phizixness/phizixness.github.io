All the Questions You Might Have Had About Photons, Answered

  • Erik
  • Nov 19, 2024
  • 6 Comments

Photons are massless particles which make up light across all wavelengths, proposed by Einstein in 1905, challenging old established belief of lights being waves. His theory, which explained the photoelectric effect, introduced the fact that light behaves both like waves and particle which later laid the foundation of quantum mechanics. Though Einstein faced much backlashes from scientists of that time, The revolutionary theory eventually got him the nobel prize in physics in 1921. This is such a great topic to start diving into the world of particle physics.

Momentum without Mass!

If Momentum P equals mass times velocity and m is zero for photons, we get a momentum of zero. But, the formula we used here is not precious and is applicable only at lower speed.

If you read typical internet articles on this topic, you would find they focus more on the "derivation" part rather than the conceptual. They say from Einstein's gtr equation we know ‚Äî

Then let's start how a scientist would think, Asking fundamental questions. What is momentum in the first place? Momentum is the ability for an object to interact with another and change the state of that. So, Does light pushes us when we expose ourselves in front? Scientists have observed in vacuum experiments that shining light on ultra-thin metallic plates caused them to bend slightly ‚Äîproving that light, despite being massless, has momentum.

Real World Examples of Photon Momentum

Comets: The tail we see of a comet is a perfect instance of light's momentum. The intense photon particles from the Sun push dust particles away from the iced rock, core of the comet, forming the iconic tail. But it's not entirely accurate to say, because solar flares and solar winds also has influence bumping the tail backwards.

CCD Cameras: CCD cameras detect photons when they collide with the sensor‚Äôs sensitive regions, transferring momentum and generating electronic signals from collision data. This makes CCDs effective in capturing quality images without distortions. Basically, They turn light into electricity and that electric signal is the momentum transferred by photons. Though CCD cameras are not a mainstream anymore, fields like astrophotography still relies on the precise data of them.

Human Eyes: In our eyes, photon collisions with photoreceptor cells create chemical changes that allow us to perceive light. Here, photon momentum initiates the process of vision.

Energy without Mass!

Photons also carry energy, which can have a significant impact on various materials and biological processes, despite photons being massless. Theoretically it is known E=PC from Einstein's equation. So if photons can have momentum they can also have energy.

Real World Examples of Photon Energy

Solar Panels: Photons in sunlight strike solar cells and transfer their energy to electrons, freeing electrons and creating an electric current.
Photosynthesis: Plants use sunlight to convert carbon dioxide and water into glucose. The energy of photons absorbed by chlorophyll drives the chemical reactions essential to plant growth.
Laser Technology: Concentrated photon energy in lasers is used to cut, weld, and engrave materials.
Ultraviolet (UV) Light and Sunburns: UV photons have high energy and can penetrate the skin, where they can cause chemical changes to DNA molecules. This energy transfer damages cells, which is what leads to sunburn and, over time, can contribute to skin aging or even skin cancer. Here, the energy carried by the UV photons is enough to break molecular bonds, showing the potentially harmful effects of photon energy.

This effect is known as gravitational lensing, which was one of the core concepts of Einstein's relativity and was later proved establishing the theory even more.

Why are photons massless?

Photons are massless because they serve as the force carriers for the electromagnetic field, allowing them to travel at the speed of light. In quantum field theory, force carriers like photons don‚Äôt have mass, which is essential for them to propagate without slowing down. If photons had mass, they would not be able to travel at light speed (Consequence of Relativity), and electromagnetic waves would behave differently.
