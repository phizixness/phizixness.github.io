All the Questions you might have about Photons, Answered.

Erik & Piyash on Oct 26, 2024

Photons are massless particles which make up light across all wavelengths, proposed by Einstein in 1905, challenging old established belief of lights being waves. His theory, which explained the photoelectric effect, introduced the fact that light behaves both like waves and particle which later laid the foundation of quantum mechanics. Though Einstein faced much backlashes from scientists of that time, The revolutionary theory eventually got him the nobel prize in physics in 1921. This is such a great topic to start diving into the world of particle physics.

Momentum without Mass!

If Momentum P equals mass times velocity and m is zero for photons, we get a momentum of zero. But, the formula we used here is not precious and is applicable only at lower speed.
If you read typical internet articles on this topic, you would find they focus more on the "derivation" part rather than the conceptual. They say from Einstein's gtr equation we know —
E²=(mc²)²+(pc)²
For photons m=0 making the equation —
E=pc
So for photons, P=E/c. Here you have it. This demonstrates photons have momentum. Just another boring physics lecture right?
Then let's start how a scientist would think, Asking fundamental questions. What is momentum in the first place? Momentum is the ability for an object to interact with another and change the state of that. So, Does light pushes us when we expose ourselves in front? Scientists have observed in vacuum experiments that shining light on ultra-thin metallic plates caused them to bend slightly —proving that light, despite being massless, has momentum.

Real World Examples of Photon Momentum

Comets: The tail we see of a comet is a perfect instance of light's momentum. Photons from the Sun push dust particles away from the comet, forming the iconic tail.
CCD Cameras: CCD cameras detect photons when they collide with the sensor’s sensitive regions, transferring momentum and generating electronic signals. This makes CCDs effective in capturing images.
Human Eyes: In our eyes, photon collisions with photoreceptor cells create chemical changes that allow us to perceive light. Here, photon momentum initiates the process of vision.

Energy without Mass!

Photons also carry energy, which can have a significant impact on various materials and biological processes, despite photons being massless.

Real World Examples of Photon Energy

Solar Panels: Photons in sunlight strike solar cells and transfer their energy to electrons, creating an electric current. The efficiency of solar panels depends on the energy of the photons hitting the cells.
Photosynthesis: Plants use sunlight to convert carbon dioxide and water into glucose. The energy of photons absorbed by chlorophyll drives the chemical reactions essential to plant growth.
Laser Technology: Concentrated photon energy in lasers is used to cut, weld, and engrave materials. High-energy photons in lasers provide a powerful tool for industrial and medical applications.
Ultraviolet (UV) Light: UV photons carry enough energy to break molecular bonds, which can be harmful to cells. This energy is used in sterilization processes and UV-based medical treatments.
Radiotherapy: High-energy photons, such as X-rays, are used in cancer treatments to target and destroy cancer cells. Photons transfer energy to cells, effectively disrupting cancer cell functions.
Particle Accelerators (e.g., CERN): In experiments at facilities like CERN, high-energy photons can interact with particles to produce new particles. This energy transfer is fundamental to studying subatomic particle behavior.

How gravity bends the path of light?

While photons have no mass, gravity can bend their path due to the warping of spacetime itself. Massive objects curve spacetime, causing photons to follow curved paths as they travel through these gravitational fields.

How can something be massless but still exist?

Photons lack rest mass but still possess energy and momentum, allowing them to interact with the world around us. Their unique properties enable them to travel at the speed of light and play a critical role in electromagnetic interactions.

Why are photons massless?

Photons are massless because they serve as the force carriers for the electromagnetic field, allowing them to travel at the speed of light. In quantum field theory, force carriers like photons don’t have mass, which is essential for them to propagate without slowing down. If photons had mass, they would not be able to travel at light speed, and electromagnetic waves would behave differently.

How do photons interact with other particles?

Photons interact through processes like absorption, emission, and scattering. For instance, in the photoelectric effect, photons transfer energy to electrons, causing them to jump to higher energy levels. Scattering changes a photon's direction after collision, which explains phenomena like the blue color of the sky (Rayleigh scattering). These interactions are key to energy transfer in technologies like solar panels, photosynthesis, and radiotherapy.

Why don’t photons experience time?

According to relativity, as particles approach the speed of light, time dilates. Since photons travel at light speed, they experience no passage of time from their own frame of reference. This means a photon emitted billions of years ago still “arrives” instantly in its own frame, highlighting the timeless nature of photons as they traverse vast distances across the universe.

How do photons produce color?

Color is produced by photons of different wavelengths within the visible spectrum. Human eyes detect these variations, interpreting longer wavelengths as red and shorter ones as blue. Objects appear colored based on the light wavelengths they reflect or emit. For example, an apple reflects red light more than other colors, making it appear red to us.

How do photons relate to temperature?

Temperature is closely tied to photon emission through thermal radiation. As objects heat up, they emit photons with higher energy. Cooler objects emit infrared light, while very hot objects like stars emit visible and ultraviolet light. This concept, known as blackbody radiation, allows scientists to determine temperatures of distant stars and planets by analyzing emitted wavelengths.

Can photons be stopped?

In a vacuum, photons cannot be stopped as they inherently travel at light speed. However, in materials like glass or water, photons slow due to interactions with atomic particles. Certain experiments in quantum optics can even “freeze” photons temporarily in special media, although this delay is due to controlled interactions, not a true stopping.

Do photons have a lifetime?

Photons are considered stable and can exist indefinitely in a vacuum. However, if photons interact with particles, such as being absorbed by an atom, they transfer their energy, effectively ending their “life.” Photons from distant stars travel across billions of years unless they interact with particles, which may scatter or absorb them.

How do photons form in the first place?

Photons are formed through mechanisms like electron transitions, nuclear reactions, and particle-antiparticle annihilation. For instance, when an electron drops to a lower energy state in an atom, it emits a photon. Nuclear reactions in stars produce vast quantities of photons, which we observe as light. Particle annihilation, like that of an electron and positron, also releases photons, demonstrating energy’s transformation into light.

What happens when two photons collide?

Normally, photons pass through each other without interacting due to their neutrality. However, at very high energies, photon-photon scattering can occur, allowing two photons to generate particle pairs. This rare event, observed in particle accelerators, is predicted by quantum electrodynamics and sheds light on particle behavior under extreme conditions.

Can we ever see individual photons?

Yes, with sensitive equipment, individual photons can be detected. Devices like photomultiplier tubes or CCD cameras, used in fields such as astronomy and quantum research, can register single photons. Observing single photons helps researchers understand faint astronomical objects and study quantum behaviors on small scales.

What is photon entanglement?

Photon entanglement is a quantum phenomenon where two photons become linked, meaning changes in one affect the other instantly, regardless of distance. This property, crucial to quantum mechanics, is foundational in quantum computing and encryption. Entangled photons demonstrate how information can be shared instantaneously across large distances, which is central to studies in quantum communication.

Why do photons travel in straight lines?

Photons generally travel in straight lines or geodesics, the straightest paths in spacetime, unless deflected by gravity or matter. This straight-line behavior is due to their masslessness and the structure of spacetime. Near massive objects, photons follow the curvature of spacetime, resulting in gravitational lensing, where light bends around stars or planets.
