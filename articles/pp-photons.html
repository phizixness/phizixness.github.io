All the Questions you might have about Photons, Answered.

Erik & Piyash on Nov 19, 2024

Photons are massless particles which make up light across all wavelengths, proposed by Einstein in 1905, challenging old established belief of lights being waves. His theory, which explained the photoelectric effect, introduced the fact that light behaves both like waves and particle which later laid the foundation of quantum mechanics. Though Einstein faced much backlashes from scientists of that time, The revolutionary theory eventually got him the nobel prize in physics in 1921. This is such a great topic to start diving into the world of particle physics.

Momentum without Mass!

If Momentum P equals mass times velocity and m is zero for photons, we get a momentum of zero. But, the formula we used here is not precious and is applicable only at lower speed.
If you read typical internet articles on this topic, you would find they focus more on the "derivation" part rather than the conceptual. They say from Einstein's gtr equation we know —
E²=(mc²)²+(pc)²
For photons m=0 making the equation —
E=pc
So for photons, P=E/c. Here you have it. This demonstrates photons have momentum. Just another boring physics lecture right?
Then let's start how a scientist would think, Asking fundamental questions. What is momentum in the first place? Momentum is the ability for an object to interact with another and change the state of that. So, Does light pushes us when we expose ourselves in front? Scientists have observed in vacuum experiments that shining light on ultra-thin metallic plates caused them to bend slightly —proving that light, despite being massless, has momentum.

Real World Examples of Photon Momentum

Comets: The tail we see of a comet is a perfect instance of light's momentum. The intense photon particles from the Sun push dust particles away from the iced rock, core of the comet, forming the iconic tail. But it's not entirely accurate to say, because solar flares and solar winds also has influence bumping the tail backwards.
CCD Cameras: CCD cameras detect photons when they collide with the sensor’s sensitive regions, transferring momentum and generating electronic signals from collision data. This makes CCDs effective in capturing quality images without distortions. Basically, They turn light into electricity and that electric signal is the momentum transferred by photons. Though CCD cameras are not a mainstream anymore, fields like astrophotography still relies on the precise data of them.
Human Eyes: In our eyes, photon collisions with photoreceptor cells create chemical changes that allow us to perceive light. Here, photon momentum initiates the process of vision.

Energy without Mass!

Photons also carry energy, which can have a significant impact on various materials and biological processes, despite photons being massless. Theoretically it is known E=PC from Einstein's equation. So if photons can have momentum they can also have energy.

Real World Examples of Photon Energy

Solar Panels: Photons in sunlight strike solar cells and transfer their energy to electrons, freeing electrons and creating an electric current.
Photosynthesis: Plants use sunlight to convert carbon dioxide and water into glucose. The energy of photons absorbed by chlorophyll drives the chemical reactions essential to plant growth.
Laser Technology: Concentrated photon energy in lasers is used to cut, weld, and engrave materials.
Ultraviolet (UV) Light and Sunburns: UV photons have high energy and can penetrate the skin, where they can cause chemical changes to DNA molecules. This energy transfer damages cells, which is what leads to sunburn and, over time, can contribute to skin aging or even skin cancer. Here, the energy carried by the UV photons is enough to break molecular bonds, showing the potentially harmful effects of photon energy.

How gravity bends the path of light?

While photons have no mass, gravity can bend their path due to the warping of spacetime itself. Massive objects curve spacetime, causing photons to follow curved paths as they travel through these gravitational fields.
This effect is known as gravitational lensing, which was one of the core concepts of Einstein's relativity and was later proved establishing the theory even more.

Why are photons massless?

Photons are massless because they serve as the force carriers for the electromagnetic field, allowing them to travel at the speed of light. In quantum field theory, force carriers like photons don’t have mass, which is essential for them to propagate without slowing down. If photons had mass, they would not be able to travel at light speed (Consequence of Relativity), and electromagnetic waves would behave differently.

Why don’t photons experience time?

According to relativity, as particles approach closer to the speed of light, time dilates. Since photons travel at light speed, they experience no passage of time from their own frame of reference. This means a photon emitted billions of years ago still “arrives” instantly in its own frame, highlighting the timeless nature of photons as they traverse vast distances across the universe. An insightful thought regarding this: Photons experience no passage of time, So if you were the photon and suppose you were born in the Sun, you would instantly discover yourself at the edge of our observable universe (Only if you're lucky enough not being absorbed or harmed anyway by any cosmic object) and thus you would die instantly from your reletive.

How do photons produce color?

Color is produced by photons of different wavelengths within the visible spectrum. Human eyes detect these variations, interpreting longer wavelengths as red and shorter ones as blue. Objects appear colored based on the light wavelengths they reflect or emit. The following color concept is well described in the article about Black Body Radiation.

How do photons relate to temperature?

Temperature is closely tied to photon emission through thermal radiation. As objects heat up, they emit photons with higher energy. Cooler objects emit infrared light, while very hot objects like stars emit visible and ultraviolet light. This concept, known as blackbody radiation, allows scientists to determine temperatures of distant stars and planets by analyzing emitted wavelengths.

How do photons form in the first place?

Photons are formed through mechanisms like electron transitions, nuclear reactions, and particle-antiparticle annihilation. For instance, when an electron drops to a lower energy state in an atom, it emits a photon. Nuclear reactions in stars produce vast quantities of photons, which we observe as light. Particle annihilation, like that of an electron and positron, also releases photons, demonstrating energy’s transformation into light.

Why do photons travel in straight lines?

Photons generally travel in straight lines or geodesics, the straightest paths in spacetime, unless deflected by gravity or matter. This straight-line behavior is due to their masslessness and the structure of spacetime.
